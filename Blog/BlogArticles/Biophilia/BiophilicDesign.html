Biophilia: flower-power your home

An argument for a greener living room

Introduction

 You might not realize how commonly people add natural elements to man-made things: the bouquet in your living room, the ‘apple’ on my laptop or the image in this article. This is what Science knows as ‘Biophilia’.

Biophilia is the theory that humans are innately attracted to the natural world; people like to approximate plants, animals and the natural environment.

Biophilia has become increasingly popular in the world of design. The world’s leading companies seek to move beyond squared architecture and indoor design. Even smaller companies ride the biophilic design wave. Take, for instance, your local café that recently added a green interior wall. They have caught on to science: indoor plants give us ‘positive vibes’.

Why should I care?

  Perhaps you are not convinced by the Biophilia theory. That’s alright: what follows is a list of small-scoped studies that all support the hypothesis from their own angle. The (linked) studies found that biophilic elements enhance:

  • retention (studying)
  • productivity
  • mood
  • overall well-being
  • mindfullness
  • ability to restore mental fatigue

Hopefully you are convinced by now, because I will tell you the best thing:
You can reap these benefits today!
The studies show that small, simple retouches in your living space can trigger the desired effects. In other words, adding some plants here and there should do the trick!

Advanced Biophilic Design

  If you think it’s worth the effort, you could redesign your home more thoroughly. These tips do get quite advanced, but perhaps they will help some of you fanatic DIYers.

  • Add biomorphic (naturally shaped / curving) elements)
  • Include elements of water
  • Stack items in fibonacci incrementsg
  • Properly light rooms, but buy dim lights
  • Avoid stagnant shadows (they don’t exist in nature)
  • Mix the complex with the plain (see feature image)

But — most of all — follow that which feels right.
